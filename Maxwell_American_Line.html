Research Summary

This page documents the American Maxwell line from Adam Maxwell (b. 1752, Cumberland Co., PA, per his sworn 1832 pension — an American-born ancestor, not the immigrant) who settled in Buffalo Township, Armstrong County, Pennsylvania, down through seven generations to the present. The immigrant ancestor is an unknown earlier figure, currently unidentified; the documented Scottish connection via the Auchincairn / Cowhill branch is established by Y-DNA but the genealogical bridge is broken (see Connection to the Scottish Line). The goal: confirm each father-to-son link with primary records rather than relying on the existing printed family chart (FT-1).

The chain is anchored at the top by Adam Maxwell's 1820 will in Armstrong County, which names his five sons; in the middle by an 1842 Orphans' Court petition (ORPH-1) naming David Maxwell's five minor children and fixing his death before 19 December 1842, and an 1849 release of estate confirming that David's widow Margaret had remarried his brother James Maxwell; and at the bottom by federal census records, Ohio death certificates, and Ohio county vital records.

The corrected chain runs through David Maxwell at generation 2 — FT-1's original identification, vindicated by the 1849 release after a brief detour through a William Maxwell hypothesis (see Dead Ends). The brother-marries-widow scenario was correct in structure but misapplied to the wrong brother: it was David → James, not William → James.

Key Findings & Clarifications

REL-1

FT-1 was right: David Maxwell at generation 2 May 2026 reconfirmation

The printed family chart (FT-1) named generation 22 as David Maxwell, b. ~1798, d. 1830, Armstrong Co., PA. Adam Maxwell's 1820 will confirms David as one of Adam's five sons. The keystone 1849 estate release (REL-1) explicitly names Sarah, Emily, and Samuel Huston Maxwell as "the Children and heirs of David Maxwell" — settling the paternity question with a contemporary instrument signed by the family members themselves.

FT-1's death year of 1830 remains incorrect — David must have lived at least until 1833 to father Samuel — but the basic identification was right. The 1910 death certificate's contradictory naming of "William Maxwell" as Samuel's father (DEATH-S1) is best understood as informant error: Mack Y. Maxwell, born 1864, was supplying the name of a grandfather who had died decades before his own birth.

Net effect: Generation 2 = David Maxwell, restored. FT-1's identification was correct; the death year is now tightened to before 19 December 1842 by the Orphans' Court record (ORPH-1), which also names all five of David's children.
ORPH-1 · REL-1

The brother-marries-widow scenario, confirmed David d. by Dec 1842 → James, by Nov 1849

Two records anchor the sequence. The 1842 Orphans' Court (ORPH-1; Armstrong Co., December Term 1842, No. 3) records the petition of "Margaret Maxwell, widow of David Maxwell late of Buffalo Tp … deceased," stating that David died leaving five children, all minors under fourteen — Sarah, Eliza Emily, Samuel [Huston], Adam, and James — with no one authorized over their persons and estates; the court appointed John Craig and William Huston guardians (bond filed 25 Jan 1843). This fixes David's death to before 19 December 1842 and names his children directly.

The 1849 release (REL-1) then describes "James Maxwell and Margaret Maxwell his wife … the Said Margaret in her right as well, as the widow of David Maxwell, deceased, as the wife of James Maxwell" — establishing that by November 1849 Margaret, David's widow, had married James Maxwell, David's brother (Adam's eldest son, named first in WILL-A1). The remarriage gave Margaret legal standing under both identities and shaped how Adam's estate was settled among the surviving heirs. It is the textbook nineteenth-century pattern: a brother taking on his deceased brother's widow and children, keeping property and family within the same patriline.

Sorting the blended household. Because David was dead by the end of 1842, his children are the five named in ORPH-1 (all born by 1842). Any children appearing later in the household who were born after 1842 — Addison (b. ~1852), and Harlow, Pauline, Robert, and William if their births post-date 1842 — are Margaret and James's joint children, not David's, and should be assigned by birth date. The composite family seen together in the 1860 Pike County census under James H. Maxwell thus combines David's five with James and Margaret's own issue.

Two further leads from ORPH-1: (1) the guardian William Huston, paired with the grandson's name Samuel Huston Maxwell, strongly suggests Margaret's maiden name was Huston (guardians were commonly maternal kin) — a probable answer to the open "Margaret's maiden name" question, and likely the true form behind the garbled "Histone/Heistand." To be confirmed by a marriage record. (2) The other guardian, John Craig, is the Craig family long associated with Adam — the Maxwell–Craig tie continuing into the next generation.
Methodological notes: the brother-marries-widow hypothesis was first proposed when the 1910 death certificate's "William" reading led to a parallel construction with the wrong brother; the structural insight was right, and REL-1 identified the correct brother (James), with ORPH-1 now anchoring David's death and family. Note also a disambiguation hazard: at least three James Maxwells are in play — Adam's son James (who married Margaret), David's minor son James (ORPH-1), and the earlier Toboyne James. A James & Margaret marriage record itself remains to be located.
DEED-1

The Maxwell–Dinsmore connection is older than the Pike County marriage Armstrong Co., PA · 1852

The 1852 deed (DEED-1) shows William Maxwell and John Maxwell (with John's wife Margaret) of Armstrong County, Pennsylvania, selling North Buffalo Township land to Robert Dinsmore. Eight years later, Samuel Houston Maxwell will marry Mary E. Dinsmore in Pike County, Ohio (MAR-1).

The two families were neighbors in Armstrong County and conducted property transactions together in the early 1850s. Their joint migration to Pike County, Ohio — and the Maxwell-Dinsmore marriage there in 1860 — was not a coincidence between strangers, but a continuation of an existing PA relationship. The migration window aligned with David's death, Margaret's remarriage, and the broader settling of Adam's estate. The family network reorganized and moved west together.

DEED-2

What happened to William, Adam's son Richland County, Ohio · 1865

The 1865 deed (DEED-2) shows William Maxwell, with wife Lovisa, of Richland County, Ohio, selling his inherited Armstrong County PA tract to his brother Adam W. Maxwell. William — Adam Sr.'s second son in WILL-A1 — survived his father by nearly thirty years and migrated north-central rather than south-central in Ohio. His presence in Richland County, alive and conducting business in 1865, is what definitively rules him out as Samuel's father (who died decades earlier as David). It also accounts in part for the likely source of Mack Y. Maxwell's confusion on the 1910 death certificate: William was the Maxwell uncle Samuel would have known of as living, while David, having died before Samuel's earliest memories, was the more easily forgotten paternal name.

What primary research confirmed, corrected, or reversed: FT-1's birth entry for Adam — "b. 1752, Cumberland Co., PA" — is vindicated by Adam's 1832 sworn pension declaration (PEN-A1), in which he states he was born in 1752 in Cumberland County, Pennsylvania. Ralph Davis Maxwell's birth year of ~1925 is slightly early — the 1940 federal census places his birth ~1922–1923.
FT-2

Ancestors — Page 2 with Handwritten Annotations ✓ Confirmed

Handwritten annotations on the second page of the family chart. Records a claimed Scottish connection: "Parents: John Maxwell 15 May 1726 Stirling Stirlingshire Scotland married 17 May 1747 Stirling Scotland / Mary Finlayson 1727" — asserting that Adam Maxwell's parents were John Maxwell of Stirling and Mary Finlayson. This annotation is most plausibly the original source of the Stirling identification rather than independent confirmation of it: it records a family tradition committed to paper in 2010 that has since been disproved (see Dead Ends).

The annotation date of 4/21/2010 establishes the state of family knowledge before the 2026 primary-source research. The notes connecting the American line to John Maxwell of Stirling (b. 1726) and Mary Finlayson represent a family tradition of long standing — but this tradition has been disproved as Adam's direct line of descent. Adam's own sworn pension places his birth in 1752 in Cumberland County, PA, not in Stirling in 1749; and the Stirling child of OPR-B0 (baptised 1749, son of John Maxwell and Mary Finlayson) was buried at Stirling on 5 December 1750, having died in infancy. FT-2 is therefore best understood as the probable origin — not confirmation — of the Stirling hypothesis that was current before 2026.

What the certificate gets wrong is the father's name: it gives "William Maxwell," but the 1849 release (REL-1), signed during Samuel's own lifetime by his step-father James and other family members, explicitly names David Maxwell as Samuel's father. Methodological note. Mack Y. Maxwell, born 1864, supplied the parental information on his father Samuel's 1910 death certificate. David Maxwell — Mack's biological grandfather — had died decades before Mack's own birth, while another Maxwell uncle, William (Adam's son, alive in Richland Co. OH until at least 1865, see DEED-2), was the William of family memory. The error is a representative case of informant unreliability on grandparental information when the grandparent died before the informant's lifetime.
MAR-1

Samuel H. Maxwell & Mary E. Dinsmore — Marriage, 27 August 1860 ✓ Confirmed

Pike County, Ohio Marriage Records · p.155, entry No. 80 · Solemnized 27 August 1860

Record No. 80: "Samuel H. Maxwell to Mary E. Dinsmore · Aug. 27th 1860."

Independent confirmation of Samuel + Mary E. Dinsmore as a couple, in Pike County, exactly eight days before the 1860 federal census (CENS-1860) enumerated Samuel still listed in his step-father's household. Catches Samuel still in his parents' household one week after his 27 August 1860 marriage to Mary E. Dinsmore. James H. The simplest explanation: Samuel married Mary E. Dinsmore on 27 August 1860 — eight days before this enumeration — and had already moved out by the time Jacob Calley walked through the township on 4 September. He still belonged to the household as of the census's official 1 June 1860 reference date, so the enumerator (or family informant) inserted him after first listing the children currently resident. Not a signal about parentage; a wedding-week artifact.

Section 4 — 19th-Century Pennsylvania & Westward: Settling Adam's Estate

REL-1 ⭐

Release of Estate — James Maxwell, Margaret, Bowser, Sarah/Emily/Samuel Houston Maxwell ✓ Confirmed · ⭐ Key

Armstrong County, PA Deed Book Vol. 19, p.277 · Acknowledged 11 November 1849, Philadelphia · Recorded 2 December 1852

The keystone document for the corrected American line. Opens:

"Know all men by these presents That We James Maxwell and Margaret Maxwell his wife, Joseph Bowser who was intermarried with Elizabeth Maxwell, one of the heirs of Adam Maxwell deceased, Sarah Maxwell, Emily Maxwell, Samuel Huston Maxwell who were the Children and heirs of David Maxwell, who was one of the heirs of Adam Maxwell deceased, the Said Margaret in her right as well, as the widow of David Maxwell, deceased, as the wife of James Maxwell..."

Signed: James Maxwell, Margaret Maxwell, Eliza E. (Emily) Maxwell, Samuel Maxwell, Sarah Maxwell, Joseph Bowser.

This single sentence establishes the entire corrected upper chain: (1) David Maxwell was a son of Adam Maxwell, deceased; (2) David himself had died by November 1849, leaving a widow Margaret; (3) Margaret had remarried James Maxwell, also a son of Adam — i.e., David's brother; (4) David and Margaret's children were Sarah, Emily, and Samuel Huston Maxwell. The middle name resolves on close reading of REL-1 as Huston — Margaret's maiden name, confirmed by DEATH-S1 which lists her as "Margaret Hustone." The use of the maternal surname as a child's middle name was standard practice in Scots-Irish Pennsylvania families. The earlier reading of "Houston" (as in Sam Houston) was a transcription variant and a chronological misattribution: Samuel was born in February 1833, nearly three years before Sam Houston's fame at San Jacinto.
DEED-1

William Maxwell & John Maxwell to Robert Dinsmore — Land Deed Armstrong Co., PA · 23 Aug 1852

Armstrong County, PA Deed Book Vol. 19, p.544 · Executed 23 August 1852 · Recorded 21 August 1857

"William Maxwell and John Maxwell and Margaret Maxwell his wife of the County of Armstrong State of Pennsylvania of the first part and Robert Dinsmore of the same county and State of the second part." The Maxwells are selling for $950 cash a tract in North Buffalo Township that had been "devised by Adam Maxwell late of the County of Armstrong, deceased, by his last will and testament." Signed: Wm Maxwell, John Maxwell, James H. Maxwell (as witness), Margaret Maxwell.

Documents the Maxwell–Dinsmore relationship in Armstrong County eight years before Samuel Houston Maxwell would marry Mary E. Dinsmore in Pike County, Ohio. The two families were not merely co-migrants — they were neighbors and business associates in PA, with property changing hands between them as Adam's estate was being settled. This recasts the 1860 Pike County marriage from a chance encounter to a continuation of an existing relationship. The deed also confirms that William, John, and Margaret/James Maxwell were among Adam's surviving heirs actively winding up his estate fifteen years after his death.

Armstrong County, PA Deed Book Vol. 19, multiple folios · 1852

A sequence of releases from Adam Maxwell's heirs, each transferring their remaining estate claims to John Maxwell and William Maxwell for the purpose of finally winding up Adam's estate fifteen years after his death. Includes:

William White, with children Eliza, Adam M., and Mahlon), and the heirs of David Maxwell (the same David named in REL-1 as Samuel Houston's father). The adjoining-landowner references repeatedly place David's heirs in possession of land in North Buffalo Township — exactly the location Adam's 1820 will gave them. Several of the releases are made in Ohio (Coshocton County), confirming that multiple Maxwell descendants had migrated to Ohio by 1852.
DEED-2

William Maxwell & Lovisa to Adam W. Maxwell — Land Deed Richland Co., OH · 13 July 1865

Armstrong County, PA Deed Book Vol. ___, p.___ · Recorded 17 July 1865 · Acknowledged before Probate Judge Milton W. Worden, Richland Co., OH

William Maxwell of Richland County, Ohio, with wife Lovisa Maxwell, sells his half-interest in a 100-acre tract in North Buffalo Township, Armstrong County, PA, to Adam W. It also tracks where Adam's son William went after leaving Armstrong County — north-central Ohio (Richland) rather than the south-central Ohio (Pike) where his brother James settled with Margaret and the children.

Section 5 — Adam Maxwell: Life Records (1776–1837)

Adam Maxwell (b. 1752, Cumberland Co., PA, per his sworn 1832 pension; d. 17 May 1837, Buffalo Township, Armstrong County, PA) is the best-documented individual in the chain. The records below span from his pre-migration origins in Toboyne Township, Cumberland County (the 1776 and 1779 tax lists — his probable home community before moving west), through his Revolutionary War service, four consecutive federal censuses, his 1820 will, and his pension declaration and roll entry — together covering nearly six decades of his life in Pennsylvania.

Life Map

Adam Maxwell — Life Movements, 1752–1837 ✓

Compiled from his 1832 pension declaration (which names his ranging posts on the Conemaugh and Loyalhanna), the 1779 Toboyne tax list, the 1783 Westmoreland transcript and 1790 census, and his 1820 will and 1837 death in Armstrong County.

The journey, in order

b. 1752 · 1779 Toboyne Twp, Cumberland (now Perry) Co. — born in the Sherman's Valley c. 1752; listed as a single freeman on the 1779 tax list.
1776–77 Westmoreland frontier — war service — Revolutionary militia service per his pension, ranging from Blacklick, on the Conemaugh and William Watson's, on the Loyalhanna; a temporary move west, after which he returned home to Toboyne.
c. 1780–1790 Derry Twp, Westmoreland Co. — the permanent move across the Alleghenies; held 200 acres by 1783 and was enumerated here in the 1790 census.
c. 1800–1837 Buffalo Twp, Armstrong Co. — settled c. 1794; wrote his will (1820), gave his pension declaration (1832), and died here 17 May 1837.

Markers are approximate; Blacklick sits at the mouth of Blacklick Creek on the Conemaugh near Blairsville, and William Watson's is placed on the Loyalhanna near Derry (its exact site is unidentified). His wartime ranging ground between the Conemaugh and the Loyalhanna is the very country he would later settle — Derry lies between the two. Solid line = permanent westward migration; dashed line = the temporary 1776–77 war-service excursion.

The Johns' absence in 1776 (and again in 1785) is the basis for treating them as the next generation rather than James's contemporaries — see ASSESS-1.

ASSESS-1

Is the Toboyne Adam our Adam? ● Probable — not proven

Two linked claims must be kept apart: (1) identity — that the Adam Maxwell among the 1779 Toboyne freemen is our Adam (the 1832 Armstrong County pensioner, b. 1752 Cumberland Co., d. 1837); and (2) parentage — that James Maxwell of Toboyne is his father. Parentage depends on identity: if the Toboyne Adam is a different man, James-as-father falls with him. The identity is therefore the load-bearing claim.

The case for the identification.
FactorWeightWhat it shows
Birth countyStrongAdam swore (1832) he was born in Cumberland Co., 1752. The Toboyne Adam is a Cumberland man — the county Adam himself named under oath.
Age / life-stageStrongBorn 1752 → ~27 in 1779, unmarried and unlanded; a freeman is exactly that status. Precise fit.
Migration timingStrongToboyne Adam present 1779, gone by 1782; our Adam in Westmoreland by 1790 (CENS-1790). The departure window matches the westward move.
FAN networkStrongestToboyne holds three families from Adam's later documented life — Shields (Capt. John Shields, his militia commander per PEN-A2), Reed (later at Slate Lick), Kincaid (his Derry land) — across war, church, and land dealings. A coincidental second Adam Maxwell would have to share Adam's entire social network.
The gaps — why it is not proven.
  • No document equates the Toboyne Adam with the Armstrong Adam; the link is wholly circumstantial.
  • "Adam Maxwell" is not a unique name; a second Adam in Cumberland County cannot be formally excluded (though the FAN overlap makes one unlikely).
  • Adam's pension has him "living in Westmoreland when called into service" (1776–77) while the tax places him in Cumberland in 1779 — reconcilable as wartime shuttling, but a reconciliation, not a confirmation.
  • The tax entry carries nothing individuating — no age, no kin, no "son of James."
Verdict — two claims at different confidence levels.

(1) Identity — that the Toboyne freeman Adam of 1779 is our Adam — remains ● probable, resting on the FAN convergence (Shields, Reed, Kincaid), the Cumberland birth, the age/life-stage fit, and the matching departure window. This is categorically firmer than the discarded Stirling identification.

(2) Parentage — that James Maxwell was Adam's father — is ● probable but unproven, and the newer records (TAX-0, TAX-2, TAX-3, EJECT-1, HIST-1) reshape the picture. They establish James as the senior Toboyne patriarch (sole Maxwell 1776 and 1785) and the Johns as the next generation (a John "fuller" by 1811; Col. John, "son of John," holding the mill by 1820 and commissioner in 1824). The most economical model is now a James-headed family with John-branch heirs: James (warrant + mill) → his son John → Col. John → heirs → Bistline 1873. The 1815 James v. John ejectment fits as an intra-family title dispute as the estate passed down.

The honest weak point: Adam appears in Toboyne only in 1779 — absent 1776, absent 1785 — and never holds land or inherits there; the mill descends entirely through the John branch. That single, bracketed appearance is consistent with Adam being an elder son who left for Westmoreland (~1780–82) before any land descended to him (the favorable reading) but is equally consistent with a transient nephew/cousin who belonged to a different household. The new records do not decide between these. They do, however, restore James to a senior, patriarchal position — which is more favorable to the father hypothesis than a contemporaries reading would be.

What would convert it to proof (in order of force):
  1. James Maxwell's own will / estate — now the single most valuable target. If James is the patriarch, his will names his children: it will either list "son Adam … of Westmoreland/Armstrong Co." (proof of parentage) or enumerate only the John branch and omit Adam (strong evidence Adam belonged to a different household). The 1787→1814 warrant-to-survey gap and his 1811/1815 activity suggest James lived into the 1810s–1820s — look in Perry County wills/administrations ~1815–1830 (Cumberland Co. if he died before 1820).
  2. The James → John descent. Establish whether the elder John (Col. John's father) was James's son via a Perry deed/will or the 1873 Bistline conveyance "from [Col. John's] heirs", which should name the heirs and show whether any Adam-branch claim ever existed.
  3. The 1815 ejectment file (James v. John, No. 59, April 1815) — pleadings may recite the chain of title and the source of each party's claim, potentially naming James's children.
  4. A direct bridge document: a church transfer certificate (Toboyne-area congregation → Westmoreland); a militia muster placing Adam in a Cumberland company; a deed linking his Westmoreland land to a Toboyne transaction.
  5. Westmoreland land/church records for Adam, ~1780–1790 — whether he arrived propertied (suggesting an inheritance from a father) or started from nothing.
  6. Ruling out a second Adam Maxwell via the Pennsylvania Archives Third Series general index (Vol. 29, K–Ref).
  7. Y-DNA from a documented descendant of the John branch (Col. John's line), tested against Kit 752085.

Assessment — Which Westmoreland Adam Maxwell is ours? ● Probable

Evidence: the 1790 Federal Census for all of Westmoreland County (USGenWeb Archives index, M–R surnames, abstracted by Lynn Beatty), read against TAX-W1 (1783), TAX-W2 (1786), and CENS-1790.

The complete county index shows only three Maxwell households in 1790: Adam (Derry), "Arobeat" / Robert (Hempfield), and William (Unity). There is exactly one Adam Maxwell in the entire county, and he is the Derry man.

The disambiguation. In 1783–86 there were clearly two Adam Maxwells (the Derry landholder and the Mount Pleasant single man, the latter continuing into Hempfield by 1786). By 1790 only one Adam remains — in Derry. The economical reading is that the Derry Adam stayed put (1783 → 1790) and is our Adam, continuous with the corrected 1790 Derry household (one adult male, no sons yet, five females — a daughters-first young family) and with the later move to Buffalo Township, Armstrong County. The Mount Pleasant / Hempfield Adam left the county (or died) before 1790 and is a collateral — a brother, cousin, or nephew, not the direct ancestor. This supersedes an earlier working doubt (floated while only the Mount Pleasant entry was in hand) that our Adam might be the landless single man: that Adam does not persist in Westmoreland, so he is not the one who continues on to Armstrong.
Collateral Maxwell men as kin / Y-DNA candidates. The single William beside the Mount Pleasant Adam in 1783 may be the Wm Maxwell of Unity Township in 1790 (Unity adjoins the Mount Pleasant/Hempfield area) — i.e., that collateral Adam's companion, who stayed in the county. With the Robert Maxwell of Hempfield (1790), these are the most concrete same-county, same-decade Maxwell men yet identified; proven male-line descendants of either would be direct comparison targets for Kit 752085.
What remains open. (1) The clean documentary confirmation of the 1783→1790 Derry link is the Derry 200-acre warrant/patent: a warrant to Adam c. 1781–83 ties the landholder to this family; a 1760s–70s (Bedford-era) warrant would mean the Derry land predates our Adam and would reopen the question. (2) The 1790 figures here come from a volunteer index (USGenWeb), worth verifying against the original census images. (3) This settles identity only — which Westmoreland Adam is ours. HIST-2

County-history corroboration: spy, Slate Lick elder, and a 386-acre tract Smith, Hist. Armstrong Co. (1883), N. Buffalo Twp

Robert Walter Smith's History of Armstrong County, Pennsylvania (Chicago: Waterman, Watkins & Co., 1883), in its North Buffalo Township chapter, records that Maxwell settled the section along the Allegheny opposite Ford City about 1794–96, and that he "was an old Indian fighter and scout and one of the first elders of Slate Lick Presbyterian Church." Both checkable claims corroborate our Adam from a source independent of his 1832 pension: the scout/spy service matches his sworn Indian-Spy duty, and the Slate Lick eldership matches the church record.

The same chapter carries more, in its land and road-petition sections: the Adam Maxwell tract of 386 acres 70 perches, bounded by the Holland–Barnett, Kelly–Campbell, Morrow–Green, Daniel Green–Ross–Boney, and William Jack tracts; his assessment on that land in 1805 ($318) and 1806 ($258); his service as township supervisor several times; and — most valuable — his own affidavit on the proposed Freeport-to-Kittanning road, in which Adam states he "was appointed a spy by the State in the last Indian wars," his "route…from the mouth of Buffalo to the mouth of Limestone on the Allegheny river." That affidavit is a second, independent self-attestation of his spy service, in a different document and decade from the pension. (These details are from elsewhere in the same chapter, relayed via a secondary transcription — confirm wording and page against the original.)

The chapter also sets Adam among a settler community thick with his known associates — James Barr (who, per the pension, selected him as a spy in 1776), David Huston (the probable family of daughter-in-law Margaret), William Barnett and Robert McKee (1779 Toboyne surnames), Samuel Beatty, Jonathan Moore, and Robert Dinsmore — the Cumberland-and-frontier network reassembling in Armstrong.

Caveats & disambiguation: a county history (1883) is secondary, but its checkable claims here are each anchored by independent records, and Adam's spy service is doubly self-attested (pension + road affidavit). Keep our Adam (Sr., d. 1837) distinct from son Adam Jr. (WILL-A1): "Adam Maxwell" appears in more than one of the chapter's lists, and later entries may be the son; the "James Maxwell" elected school director in 1847 is a later-generation descendant. Citation note: the volume is firmly Smith's 1883 edition (per its title page); the "Ford City" locator is anomalous, since that borough dates only to 1887–89 — a curiosity worth a footnote, not a change to the citation.

PEN-A1 ⭐

Adam Maxwell — Pension Declaration, Court of Common Pleas, Armstrong County, PA ✓ Confirmed · ⭐ Key

Case No. 11221 · Sworn 19 September 1832 · Court of Common Pleas, Armstrong County, Pennsylvania · Pension Act of June 7, 1832 · Signed: Adam Maxwell (his mark) · Witness: John Craig of Buffalo Township

On 19 September 1832, Adam Maxwell — then aged eighty and a resident of Buffalo Township, Armstrong County — appeared before the Court of Common Pleas to swear his pension declaration under the Act of Congress of June 7, 1832. In the declaration Adam states he was born in 1752 in Cumberland County, Pennsylvania, adding that he had "no record of my age but was informed of it by my parents" — a first-person sworn statement under oath, and the authoritative evidence for his birth. He then describes two distinct periods of Revolutionary War service.

First service — New Jersey militia, fall 1776 or 1777. Adam volunteered under Captain John Proctor (colonel: Pomeroy; major: Alexander Barr; first lieutenant: James Moore; Adam himself second lieutenant) while living in Westmoreland County, Pennsylvania. The regiment marched to Woodbridge, New Jersey, which served as their headquarters, with the British quartered at Amboy or Brunswick about three miles distant and General Washington's headquarters at Morristown. During their six-month tour they conducted repeated skirmishes around Woodbridge, preventing British troops from plundering the local farms. On one occasion the British advanced in force, driving the militia back to Ash Swamp, where they were reinforced by "General Maxwell" (almost certainly Brigadier General William Maxwell, the New Jersey Continental commander known as "Scotch Willie") and fought a successful rearguard action that compelled the British to retreat. After the six months expired the regiment marched to Washington's headquarters, was directed to Philadelphia for their pay, and was discharged by their colonel. Adam then returned to Westmoreland County. His commission as lieutenant and his discharge papers had been lost or destroyed, as he had taken no particular care of them.

Second service — Indian Spy, 1776. Adam was selected by General Charles Campbell and James Barr from the militia ranks to serve as an Indian Spy. His party included Captain Joseph Eagers, Benjamin Leisure, John Leisure, Captain Jeremiah Lochry, John Cooper, and himself. Their route ran via Blacklicks on the Conemaugh and William Watson's on the Loyalhanna, exchanging intelligence about Indian movements with other spy parties along the way — intelligence that was then published to the inhabitants. He served several months in this capacity. Corroboration by John Craig. John Craig of Buffalo Township, Armstrong County, aged eighty, swore a supporting affidavit: he well knew Adam Maxwell, believed him to be of the stated age, recollected that Adam was a volunteer and lieutenant as claimed, and confirmed that his own brother Alexander Craig had served in the same regiment. Craig testified that Adam and Alexander marched together from Westmoreland County in 1777 to join Washington's army in New Jersey, departed in the fall, and returned the following spring. He had often heard his brother Alexander say that Adam Maxwell was at the Battle of Ash Swamp with him. Craig also affirmed his belief that Adam's Indian Spy service was true.

Genealogical significance. Most importantly: Adam's sworn statement places his birth in 1752 in Cumberland County, Pennsylvania — not in Scotland. He is therefore not the immigrant ancestor; the immigration happened at least one generation earlier. The declaration also confirms Adam as a resident of Buffalo Township, Armstrong County in 1832, consistent with his 1820 will (WILL-A1) and FT-2's note of Indian Spy and 2nd PA Militia service at Morristown, NJ. His stated age of eighty in September 1832 corresponds to a birth year of ~1752 — entirely consistent with his own sworn statement. The "General Maxwell" reinforcing at Ash Swamp is almost certainly not a close relative: Brigadier General William "Scotch Willie" Maxwell commanded the New Jersey Continentals in this theatre. The Craig affidavit independently anchors Adam to Westmoreland County in 1777 and confirms the Ash Swamp engagement — corroboration that cannot be explained as copying Adam's own testimony. Together these pages constitute the richest first-person account of Adam Maxwell's life in Pennsylvania.

Dead Ends & Superseded Hypotheses

Genealogical research is iterative. Two superseded hypotheses are documented here so that future researchers can see the reasoning trail and avoid retreading either dead end.

DEATH-S1

✗ William Maxwell as Samuel's Father superseded May 2026

The hypothesis: Samuel Huston Maxwell's father was William Maxwell — Adam's son, brother of James — based on the 1910 death certificate (DEATH-S1) naming William as Samuel's father. Under this reading, William would have died between ~1852 and 1860, after which his widow Margaret remarried his brother James.

The disproof: Two independent contemporary documents make the William reading impossible:

  • REL-1 (1849) explicitly names "Sarah Maxwell, Emily Maxwell, Samuel Huston Maxwell who were the Children and heirs of David Maxwell" — not William.
  • DEED-2 (1865) shows William Maxwell alive and conducting business in Richland County, Ohio — therefore not the deceased Maxwell father of any timeline before 1865.

The lesson: Death certificate parental information is reliable for the deceased's spouse and immediate family but unreliable for grandparents who died before the informant's lifetime. Mack Y. Maxwell, born 1864, never met his biological grandfather David (deceased before 1849) and supplied the name of an uncle (William, of Richland Co., OH) instead. The error is representative of a wider class of informant unreliability on grandparental information, and should be treated as a documented case study, not a special anomaly.

Note for future researchers: If you encounter DEATH-S1 first and see "William Maxwell" as father, you may be tempted to look for a deceased William in Armstrong County, PA in the 1850s. Don't. Look for David, and consult REL-1 in Armstrong County Deed Book Vol. 19, p.277. The starting point for this whole research thread was FT-1, which correctly named David — the 1910 death certificate was the outlier, not the rule.
OPR Deaths 1750

✗ Adam Maxwell = Stirling 1749 baptism / immigrant ancestor superseded June 2026

The hypothesis: Adam Maxwell (generation 1) was the child baptised on 20 August 1749 in the Stirling Associate Congregation as son of John Maxwell and Mary Finlayson (OPR-B0 / CH3/559/17), and was therefore the Scottish immigrant who settled in Pennsylvania. Under this reading, FT-1's "b. 1752, Cumberland Co., PA" was a family-tradition error corrected by the Scottish OPR record, and FT-2's 2010 annotation naming John Maxwell (b. 1726 Stirling) and Mary Finlayson as Adam's parents was independent confirmation.

The disproof: Two independent primary sources make the identification impossible:

  • Stirling OPR Deaths 1750 records the burial of "Adam Maxwell, son to John Maxwell, hammerman, burgess, buried at the foot of Deacon Finlayson's throughstone" on 5 December 1750. The Stirling child of OPR-B0 died in infancy, sixteen months after his baptism. He cannot be the Pennsylvania ancestor.
  • PEN-A1 (1832): Adam Maxwell swore under oath that he was born in 1752 in Cumberland County, Pennsylvania, with "no record of my age but was informed of it by my parents." A first-person sworn statement given in open court is the strongest available evidence for a living person's self-knowledge; it cannot be reconciled with a 1749 Stirling birth.

The probable source of the error: FT-2 — the 2010 annotation by David B. Maxwell recording the Stirling parentage — is most plausibly the origin of the hypothesis, not its confirmation. The annotation records a family tradition that had circulated before any primary-source checking was done. Once the Scottish OPR for the 1749 baptism was located, the tradition appeared to be confirmed; the 1750 burial record, which disproves it, was not identified until June 2026.

Note for future researchers: If you encounter OPR-B0 (CH3/559/17) first and see an Adam Maxwell baptised at Stirling in 1749 to John Maxwell and Mary Finlayson, do not assume this is the Pennsylvania ancestor. Check the 1750 Stirling burial register: the same child is buried on 5 Dec 1750. Then read Adam's own pension declaration (PEN-A1): he states clearly that he was born in 1752 in Cumberland County, Pennsylvania. The Y-DNA connection to the Cowhill / Auchincairn branch is real, but the genealogical bridge must be found in a different individual — Adam's father or grandfather, identity currently unknown.

Open Questions & Next Steps

With the brother-marries-widow scenario confirmed by REL-1 and both dead-end hypotheses closed, the primary research frontier has shifted to the generation before Adam: identifying the unknown immigrant ancestor and reconnecting the American line to the documented Scottish patriline via Y-DNA. The downstream questions (items 3 and beyond) remain valid detail-work.

  1. Identify Adam's father — and resolve James-vs-collateral. Adam was born in 1752 in Cumberland County, PA. Leading candidate: James Maxwell, the senior Maxwell patriarch of Toboyne Township (sole Maxwell there in 1776 and 1785; warrant + fulling mill 1787–1811; litigant 1815) — see TAX-0, TAX-1, WARR-1, TAX-3, HIST-1, and the assessment at ASSESS-1. The documented Toboyne succession runs James → John → Col. John → heirs (to Bistline, 1873); Adam is not in that line of inheritance, and he appears in Toboyne only once (freeman, 1779), absent 1776 and 1785. He is therefore plausibly an early-departing son of James, but a nephew/cousin relationship is not excluded. New keystone targets (in priority order): (a) James Maxwell's own will/estate — Perry Co. ~1815–1830, Cumberland Co. if before 1820; (b) the James → John descent via the 1873 Bistline "heirs" conveyance; (c) the 1815 James v. John ejectment file (No. 59), which may recite title and parentage; (d) Westmoreland land/church records for Adam ~1780–1790. The James–Nancy Brown marriage (DEED-J1) is a side thread that may distinguish an elder James from a younger one.
  2. Confirm the 1783→1790 Derry link and trace the collateral Westmoreland Maxwells. Pull the Derry 200-acre warrant/patent (PA Land Office warrant registers and copied surveys, via PHMC digital land records) — its date and warrantee confirm whether the Derry landholder of TAX-W1 is our Adam (warrant c. 1781–83) or an older, different man (1760s–70s). Separately, trace William Maxwell of Unity and Robert Maxwell of Hempfield (1790) as kinsmen / Y-DNA candidates for Kit 752085, and follow the collateral single Adam of Mount Pleasant/Hempfield out of the county. Verify the 1790 county figures against the original census images (the USGenWeb index is a volunteer abstract).
  3. Bridge the American line to Scotland via Y-DNA and colonial records. FTDNA Kit 752085 sits firmly on R-FTA17177 / Cowhill–Auchincairn. The immigrant Maxwell — Adam's father or grandfather — most likely arrived in Pennsylvania in the 1720s–1740s (consistent with Adam's 1752 Cumberland County birth). Search strategies: Pennsylvania colonial immigration records and naturalization petitions for Maxwell arrivals 1720–1755; Y-DNA matches on Kit 752085 for closer cousins who might have documented their own line back to Scotland; OPR cross-searches for Maxwell men departing Stirlingshire or Dumfriesshire in that era. Adam appears probable in Derry Township, Westmoreland County in 1790 (CENS-1790) and confirmed in Buffalo Township, Armstrong County by 1820.   5. 1850 Federal Census. Where was the combined household in 1850? James and Margaret were already married by November 1849 per REL-1, so in 1850 they should appear together — likely still in Armstrong County, PA. The household would include the older Maxwell children (Samuel Huston age 17, plus James, Harlow, Pauline, Robert, William, possibly Sarah and Emily if still single). Pre-migration baseline.
  6. David Maxwell's death and probate record. David died between Samuel's birth (early 1833) and REL-1 (Nov 1849). Armstrong County orphans' court records, intestate administration files, or a will if testate, would name David's heirs precisely and likely date his death to within a year. This is the highest-value next document. FT-1's "d. 1830" suggests there may be a known Maxwell death record in Armstrong Co. that early — possibly correct in person but mis-attributed in year, or possibly a different David.
  7. Marriage of James Maxwell and Margaret (Maxwell, widowed). The substance of the marriage is established by REL-1, but the marriage record itself remains to be located. Armstrong County or nearby PA counties, 1847–1849. Bride: Margaret Maxwell (her widowed name).
  8. Marriage of David Maxwell and Margaret Huston. Probably ~1827–1832 in Armstrong County or nearby. The Huston surname is common in the Scots-Irish communities of western Pennsylvania, consistent with the Maxwell / Craig neighborhood in Armstrong County.
